Your 18-Week Build

Building a discussion culture from scratch.

Six phases across an 18-week semester. Start with norms (not protocols), install foundational habits in the first month, and layer toward student-led discussion by week eighteen.

The hardest part is not the first week — it's resisting the urge to skip ahead. The evidence is consistent: teachers who rush past foundational habits get shallow Socratic Seminars in November. Teachers who go slow in September have rich ones in January.

What to say on day one. “In this class, we learn by talking to each other about hard questions. That means I'll be asking you to listen, write, and share ideas more than memorize facts. It takes practice, and we'll work up to it together.”

01
Weeks 1–2 · Norms

Set the classroom culture before any content.

Norms first. Content second. Protocols third.

The first two weeks are about establishing what kind of classroom this is. Co-create norms with students. Practice warm-calling without academic pressure. Introduce the quick-write habit using low-stakes prompts.

What to install

  • Co-created discussion norms in week one. Students list what makes a good discussion, what kills one. Post them.
  • Warm-calling practice with get-to-know-you prompts. Students experience the structure before the stakes are academic.
  • Daily quick-write (even 2 minutes) with social-topic prompts: “What does a fair classroom look like?”

If you only do one thing

Get the norms posted and refer to them. Every other move in this plan depends on them being visible.

02
Weeks 3–5 · Foundation

Install the rehearsal habit.

Quick-write, turn-and-talk, exit ticket. Every day.

Now academic content enters the picture. Every day begins with a quick-write, includes at least one turn-and-talk, and ends with an exit ticket you actually read.

What to install

  • Quick-write → turn-and-talk as the default opener.
  • Exit tickets read the same evening, used to open the next day.
  • Warm-calling on academic content now.
  • Two sentence frames introduced in week five: “I want to build on…” and “I see it differently because…”

Signs it's working

  • Previously silent students speaking in pairs
  • Students writing full time without prompting
  • Exit tickets showing substantive thought
03
Weeks 6–9 · Structure

Layer accountability and visual thinking.

Trios with roles, more sentence frames, See-Think-Wonder.

Small groups get structure. Document analysis becomes a trio task with assigned roles. Visual sources get See-Think-Wonder. Sentence frames expand.

What to install

  • Trios with assigned roles (Sourcer, Evidencer, Connector) for at least one doc analysis per week.
  • See-Think-Wonder as the visual-source opener.
  • Two more sentence frames: asking for evidence; paraphrasing.
  • Paraphrase accountability: after pair talks, call on students to share what their partner said.
04
Weeks 10–13 · First Protocols

Your first real protocols.

Fishbowl, Chalkboard Splash, Connect-Extend-Challenge.

With foundation in place, introduce structured protocols. Start with Fishbowl — it lets students watch discussion get modeled by peers. Run it 2–3 times over these four weeks.

What to install

  • First Fishbowl on a genuinely contested historical question.
  • Chalkboard Splash for whole-class formative assessment.
  • Connect-Extend-Challenge as an opener on day 2+ of a unit.
05
Weeks 14–17 · Synthesis

Structured Academic Controversy & conceptual change.

Argue both sides. Change your mind on purpose.

Now add SAC for content with legitimate multiple perspectives. The switch-sides step is what distinguishes SAC from debate. Pair with “I Used to Think… Now I Think…” reflections.

What to install

  • Your first full SAC over 2–3 class periods.
  • “I Used to Think… Now I Think…” as a closing reflection.
  • Student-generated questions for discussions, not only teacher-generated ones.
06
Week 18 · Seminar

First Socratic Seminar.

Teacher silent. Students facilitating. A text worth sitting with.

Seventeen weeks of scaffolded rehearsal has built the conditions for real student-led seminar. Pick a text with moral weight and ambiguity. Pull your chair outside the circle and say as little as humanly possible. Close the semester with written reflection on growth.

Your 10-Week Reset

A ramp-up starting the Monday after spring break.

Your students already have norms, relationships, and habits — some productive, some not. This plan uses spring break as a natural inflection point to layer in one habit at a time, anchored to the content you're actually teaching.

Be honest with students on day one back. Try something close to: “Over the break I've been reading research on how to make our class discussions richer. A lot of what I read said I've been putting you on the spot without giving you enough time to think. I want to try some new routines with you for the rest of the year.”

Pick one phase at a time. If a week gets eaten by assemblies, testing, or snow days, hold steady on the current phase. The point is habit formation, not coverage.

01
Weeks 1–2 · Reset

Install the rehearsal habit.

Three moves, every day, no exceptions.

The goal isn't to roll out new protocols. It's to make three practices automatic: a silent quick-write to open, a partner talk before any whole-class share, and an exit ticket to close.

What to install

  • Daily quick-write → turn-and-talk (3 min write, 2 min pair).
  • Warm-calling reframe on day one back: ask the question first, pause 5–10 seconds, then name a student.
  • Daily exit ticket. Read them. Open the next day with what you found.

Content anchor · Age of Jackson

Indian Removal is one of the most discussion-rich topics in 8th grade US history and a natural fit for the reset phase.

Sample prompts

  • What does it mean to be a “common man” in 1828? Who gets left out?
  • If you were a Cherokee leader in 1830, what would you do after the Indian Removal Act?
  • Was Jackson a democrat, a tyrant, or both? One sentence, one piece of evidence.
  • Whose voice is missing from today's lesson?

Signs it's working

  • Students writing the full 2–3 minutes without prompting.
  • Pair talk audible across the room.
  • Exit tickets showing substantive thought.
  • Previously silent students contributing in pairs.

Watch out for

  • Skipping the write step when rushed. Don't.
  • Reverting to “who wants to share?”
  • Exit tickets you never read.
  • Introducing a second routine too early.

Minimum viable version

Hold the quick-write and exit ticket, even at 60 seconds each. Drop the partner talk before you drop the write.

02
Weeks 3–4 · Structure

Layer accountability onto talk.

Sentence frames and small-group roles.

What to install

  • Two posted sentence frames: “I want to build on what ___ said because…” and “I see it differently because…”
  • Trios with assigned roles for one document-analysis task per week. Rotate every session.
  • Paraphrase accountability: after pair talks, ask for what the partner said.

Content anchor · antebellum reform movements

Abolition, women's rights, temperance, education, prison reform. Multiple perspectives built in — perfect for trio roles.

Sample trio task

  • Three sources: Garrison, Grimké, Douglass. Sourcer traces context, Evidencer pulls claims, Connector maps overlaps.
  • Seneca Falls Declaration vs. Declaration of Independence — what's changed, what's kept?
  • Who was this reform movement for? Who was it not for?
03
Weeks 5–6 · First Protocol

Your first Fishbowl.

Let them see what discussion actually looks like.

What to install

  • One Fishbowl per week, ~25 minutes.
  • See-Think-Wonder for visual sources.
  • Two more sentence frames (ask for evidence; paraphrase).

Content anchor · Manifest Destiny

Possibly the single best unit in 8th grade for discussion. Multiple legitimate perspectives, a contested phrase, visible historiography.

Fishbowl prompt candidates

  • Was Manifest Destiny inevitable?
  • Whose story does the phrase “Manifest Destiny” erase?
  • Is Gast's American Progress a celebration, a justification, or a confession?
04
Weeks 7–8 · Controversy

SAC on the Mexican-American War.

Argue one side. Argue the other. Find common ground.

What to install

  • Full SAC protocol over 2–3 class periods.
  • Two curated source packets (Side A: justified; Side B: not justified).
  • Written consensus statement as the culminating artifact.

Central question

  • Was the United States justified in going to war with Mexico in 1846?

Source suggestions

  • Polk's War Message to Congress (1846)
  • Lincoln's Spot Resolutions (1847)
  • Excerpts from Mexican Views of the War (Alcaraz et al.)
  • Thoreau, “Civil Disobedience” (1849)
  • Grant's later memoir reflection on the war
05
Weeks 9–10 · Seminar & Reflect

First Socratic Seminar, plus reflection.

Teacher silent. Students facilitating.

What to install

  • One Socratic Seminar (prep day + seminar day + reflection day).
  • Two smaller seminars of 12–15 rather than one big one.
  • End-of-year reflection on growth as a discussing class member.

Text candidates

  • Frederick Douglass, “What to the Slave is the Fourth of July?” (1852) — abridged
  • Lincoln-Douglas debate excerpts (1858)
  • Harriet Jacobs, Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl (1861)
  • Sojourner Truth, “Ain't I a Woman?” (1851) paired with Declaration of Sentiments

Closing reflection prompt

  • Think about yourself as a talker and listener in April. Think about yourself now. What changed? What didn't? What do you want to keep building next year?

One honest note about testing season

Spring testing will interrupt this plan. When it does, don't advance the phase — just hold. Habit formation beats calendar adherence.

Your Protocol Rotation

One protocol per month, refined over time.

Your foundational routines are already in place. This rotation adds breadth and depth without churning through novelties for their own sake.

Pick one protocol per month and repeat it at least twice. Single-exposure protocols feel like activities; repeated protocols become practices. The second time you run a Fishbowl, students know the structure and can focus on content.

Reflect explicitly. After each protocol, build in 5–10 minutes of “how did we do?” Student noticing is what transfers.

01
Month 1 · Fishbowl

The observation-based discussion.

Small inner circle. Outer circle with a job.

Start with Fishbowl because it legitimizes watching as a way of participating. Run it twice this month: once with a clear binary question, once with an open interpretive one.

Moves to try

  • Tap-in Fishbowl: outer-circle students swap into the inner circle when they're ready (see Facing History's Plessy demo in the Fishbowl strategy card).
  • 4-Chair variant: just four chairs in the center; talking only happens there.
  • Double Fishbowl: outer circle observes a specific inner partner, debriefs with them after.
02
Month 2 · SAC

Structured Academic Controversy.

Argue both sides. Then find consensus.

SAC is the gold standard for controversial historical content. Pick a question historians actually argue about, not a false-balance issue.

Content candidates

  • Was Reconstruction a success or a failure?
  • Was the New Deal socialist, capitalist, or neither?
  • Should the U.S. have entered WWI? WWII before Pearl Harbor?
  • Was the atomic bombing of Hiroshima/Nagasaki militarily necessary?
03
Month 3 · Socratic Seminar

Student-led text-based inquiry.

Teacher silent. Students ask, build, cite.

If you've done SAC successfully, your students can handle Socratic Seminar. The discipline is harder — no assigned sides, no time pressure, no consensus requirement. Just a text and each other.

Variants to vary

  • Inside-outside: half the class in the seminar, half observing assigned peers.
  • Two parallel seminars of 12–15 each, you walk between them.
  • Student-generated questions: students bring their own opening questions.
04
Month 4 · Microlab + Debate Team Carousel

Equal-airtime and rotating-perspective protocols.

Structured silence. Structured movement.

Microlab enforces equal airtime through timed silence. Debate Team Carousel (from the Himmeles) rotates students through multiple perspectives on a claim.

When to reach for each

  • Microlab: after a heavy-content day, for community and reflection.
  • Debate Team Carousel: when you want every student to wrestle with multiple angles on the same claim.
05
Month 5 · Student-Led

Hand over discussion leadership.

Students facilitate. You observe.

By month five, students can run routines themselves. Rotate a “discussion leader” role. Students generate the opening question, set the norms reminder, and call on classmates. You sit in the back.

06
Month 6 · Refinement & Reflection

Polish, reflect, consolidate.

Deepen the protocols that worked best.

Don't add a sixth protocol. Return to the two or three that worked best and deepen them. Close with student self-assessment and a whole-class meta-discussion about what your discussion culture has become.

Your Full-Year Arc

A discussion-centered year from day one.

Four quarters. Each builds on the last. The first week does more work than any other — protect it. Discussion habits established in the first five days will sustain the whole year.

The first week is not a syllabus-and-procedures week. It's a discussion-habits week. Run the rules lightly. Spend real time on co-creating talk norms, modeling quick-writes, and practicing warm-calling with low-stakes prompts. That's what your students will remember in October.

Q1
Weeks 1–9 · Foundation

Habits before protocols.

Quick-write, turn-and-talk, exit ticket. Every day.

Week 1 specifically

  • Day 1: co-create discussion norms with students. Post them.
  • Day 2: model quick-write and turn-and-talk with a low-stakes prompt.
  • Day 3: introduce warm-calling explicitly. Practice with get-to-know-you questions.
  • Day 4: introduce exit tickets. Read them that night. Open day 5 with what you found.
  • Day 5: first academic content. Run the full three-move rhythm.

Across Q1

  • Rehearsal-before-share as the default.
  • Two sentence frames posted by week 5, two more by week 9.
  • First See-Think-Wonder opener by week 4.
  • First trios with roles by week 7.
Q2
Weeks 10–18 · Structure

Small groups and first protocols.

Trios become reliable. Fishbowl enters.

Q2 adds structure to small groups and introduces the first protocol. Fishbowl is the right first choice — it lets students see discussion modeled by peers.

Install

  • Trios with roles, one doc analysis per week.
  • First Fishbowl by week 12.
  • Second and third Fishbowls by semester end.
  • Chalkboard Splash as a whole-class assessment.
Q3
Weeks 19–27 · Controversy

SAC becomes routine.

At least two full SACs. The switch-sides move becomes familiar.

Q3 is when SAC should become a recognizable pattern for your students. Run at least two full SACs with legitimately contested content. The first will feel mechanical; the second should feel generative.

Q4
Weeks 28–36 · Student-Led

Socratic Seminar and hand-off.

By year's end, students can run a seminar without you.

Q4 is the payoff. With three quarters of scaffolded rehearsal, students can sustain a genuine student-led Socratic Seminar. Run one early in Q4 and another late, with an end-of-year written reflection on growth.

Quick-Write → Turn-and-Talk

The workhorse. Guarantees every student has something to say before public share.

Research & how it works
What the research says

Mundelsee & Jurkowski's 2021 study of 481 ninth graders found students who had time to think and talk with a peer before whole-class sharing were 1.7x more likely to volunteer than students asked to respond immediately, and reported lower anxiety.Mundelsee & Jurkowski (2021), summary

When it works / struggles

Works: every day, every content. Struggles: closed-ended prompts. Use prompts that invite a stance.

Step-by-step
  1. Post a prompt tied to the day's lesson.
  2. 3 min silent writing. Visible timer.
  3. 2 min turn-and-talk: partner A first (60s), partner B paraphrases & adds. Switch.
  4. Warm-call, asking what their partner said.

See · Think · Wonder

Observation-before-interpretation routine from Harvard Project Zero.

Research & how it works
What the research says

Project Zero's Visible Thinking research reports gains on state and district tests in reading, writing, and social studies in classrooms using thinking routines consistently. STW specifically builds the observation-before-interpretation habit central to historical thinking.

When it works

Any day with a compelling image, artifact, political cartoon, painting, or map. Especially strong for Early Republic and antebellum content.

Step-by-step
  1. Project a single image for silent viewing (60 seconds).
  2. See: observable details only (2 min).
  3. Think: interpretation grounded in observation.
  4. Wonder: questions for inquiry.
  5. Bridge into content with the Wonder questions.

Chalkboard Splash

Silent, whole-class formative assessment made visible to everyone.

Research & how it works

A signature Total Participation Technique from the Himmeles. Gets 100% of students' thinking on the board without anyone speaking first.

Step-by-step
  1. Single open-ended prompt.
  2. Students write a response on a sticky or quarter sheet.
  3. Silently post on the board.
  4. Silent circulation — students read for patterns.
  5. Open the floor: “What did you notice?”

Connect · Extend · Challenge

A Project Zero routine that makes shifting understanding visible.

Research & how it works

Another Project Zero routine, designed to surface how students' thinking evolves across a unit. Especially powerful in history where prior conceptions are often partial.

Step-by-step
  1. Connect: How does today connect to yesterday?
  2. Extend: What new direction does it push?
  3. Challenge: What's still confusing?
  4. Use the Challenge responses as gold for discussion.

Think-Pair-Share with Accountability

The classic — but the “share” move is where most teachers lose it.

Research & how it works
What the research says

Developed by Frank Lyman at Maryland, 1981. The most-studied discussion protocol in K-12 research. Cooper et al. (2021) flag that the “Share” tends to be dominated by the same voices unless teachers explicitly structure accountability.Cooper et al. (2021)

The accountability fix

Don't ask “who wants to share?” Ask: “What did your partner say?”

Step-by-step
  1. Think: silent write, 2–3 min.
  2. Pair: 2 min. Partner A first, B paraphrases & adds. Switch.
  3. Share: warm-call, asking for the partner's ideas.

Accountable Talk Sentence Frames

Zwiers' five conversation skills + GLAD's guided oral practice.

Research & how it works

Zwiers & Crawford identify five core skills: elaborating, clarifying, supporting with evidence, building on / challenging, paraphrasing. Core to GLAD's Guided Oral Practice. Research shows significant gains in vocabulary and oral output for all students.

Starter frames to post
  • Build on: “I want to build on what ___ said. I think…”
  • Disagree: “I see it differently because…”
  • Ask for evidence: “What in the source makes you think that?”
  • Paraphrase: “So what I hear you saying is…”
  • Clarify: “Can you say more about what you mean by…?”

How to install: two frames at a time, not five. Model them yourself. Tally uses to gamify. Add two more after two weeks.

Trios with Assigned Roles

Three students, three roles, one shared artifact. No hiding.

Research & how it works

Elizabeth Cohen's Complex Instruction at Stanford showed that assigning roles and “assigning competence” widens who speaks. Trios beat quads in middle school because there's nowhere to hide.

Three roles for document analysis
  • Sourcer: who wrote this, when, why
  • Evidencer: what does the source say
  • Connector: how does it fit with the others
Step-by-step
  1. Form trios. Assign roles. Rotate every session.
  2. Each student reads all sources but analyzes through their role's lens.
  3. Group produces a single shared graphic organizer.
  4. Debrief: groups report what they learned from one groupmate's role.

Structured Academic Controversy (SAC)

Argue one side. Then argue the other. Drop both and find common ground.

Research & how it works

Developed by David and Roger Johnson at Minnesota; refined for social studies by Walter Parker at UW over three decades. The switch-sides step is what distinguishes SAC from debate.Parker, SAC: What it can be

Step-by-step (groups of 4, A-B / C-D pairs)
  1. Teacher provides a binary question and two source packets (15–20 min reading).
  2. Pair A-B prepares Side A. Pair C-D prepares Side B. (5–10 min)
  3. Pair A-B presents. C-D paraphrases before presenting their own.
  4. Switch sides. A-B now argues Side B using C-D's evidence. The critical move.
  5. Drop positions. Reach refined consensus.

Expert Groups / Jigsaw

Students become experts, then teach peers.

Research & how it works

A signature GLAD strategy and one of Slavin's most-cited cooperative learning structures. Powerful interdependence combined with deep accountability.

Step-by-step
  1. Form home groups of 3–4. Number off.
  2. All the 1s meet as Expert Group A, 2s as B, etc.
  3. Experts research together and build a shared explanation.
  4. Experts return to home groups and teach.
  5. Synthesis via quiz or randomly-selected presenter.

Warm-Calling

Cold-calling works — but only when framed warmly and preceded by rehearsal.

Research & how it works

A 2022 observational study of 86 cold-call events across 15 middle school teachers found the same technique produces opposite outcomes depending on framing. Warm: richer discussions, students safe to take risks. Catch-you-out: superficial engagement, anxiety.Edutopia summary

The four-step protocol
  1. Frame it publicly: “I call on people by name because I want to hear from everyone. I'll always give you time to think first.”
  2. Question before name. Naming first causes everyone else to disengage.
  3. Pause 5–10 seconds (Rowe's wait-time research).
  4. If they pass, let them “phone a friend” and come back for a paraphrase.

Discussion-Worthy Questions

Most questions are quiz questions. Only a few are discussion-worthy.

Three features of real discussion prompts
  • Real stakes — something to argue about
  • Textual grounding — evidence can be marshaled
  • Multiple defensible answers — no single “right” one
Strong examples
  • Was Thomas Jefferson a hypocrite?
  • Should the U.S. have recognized Haitian independence in 1804?
  • Was the Monroe Doctrine protection or imperialism?
  • Did the Compromise of 1850 prevent war or make it inevitable?
Weak examples (save for quiz)
  • When was the Constitution ratified?
  • What were the three branches of government?

Fishbowl

Small inner circle discusses. Outer circle observes with a job.

Research & how it works

A core Facing History protocol. Makes the “audience” the majority and the discussion group a small circle — useful for students still building discussion confidence. The outer circle needs a job.

Step-by-step
  1. Inner circle: 6–8 students, discuss 10–12 minutes.
  2. Outer circle: each observes one inner-circle student.
  3. Inner discusses. Outer tracks discussion moves.
  4. Swap. New inner group.
  5. Debrief: observer partners share what they noticed.

Socratic Seminar

Student-led inquiry around a shared text. Teacher silent.

Research & how it works

Favorable when well-scaffolded: students prepare a text, follow norms, teacher stays silent. A Cal State study of AVID middle-school seminars found significant gains in self-awareness, social awareness, and peer connectedness.

Step-by-step
  1. Prep day: students annotate + prepare 2–3 questions.
  2. Come prepped or don't participate.
  3. Circle setup. Teacher outside. Teacher does not call on anyone.
  4. Post norms. Open with one question. Let silence happen.
  5. Your only moves: tally participation, redirect if norms slip.
  6. Reflect in writing afterward.
Middle-school variant

Two smaller seminars of 12–15 each. You walk between them.

Exit Tickets (done right)

A 3-minute formative snapshot. Read them tonight. Open tomorrow with what you found.

Research & how it works

A variant of Angelo & Cross's “Minute Paper.” Iteratively-designed exit tickets improve both student metacognition and teacher responsiveness.

Strong prompt types
  • Application: “If Toussaint were alive in 1820s Mexico, which side would he take?”
  • Connection: “How does today connect to Unit 6?”
  • Stance with evidence: “Do you agree the Whiskey Rebellion was a failure? One sentence.”
  • Question generation: “What's one question you still have?”
Step-by-step
  1. Single focused prompt in the last 3–4 min.
  2. Students respond on sticky, index card, or digital form.
  3. Read that evening. Sort: got it / partial / misconception.
  4. Open the next day with what you found.

I Used to Think… Now I Think…

A Project Zero routine to capture shifts in understanding.

Research & how it works

Designed to make students aware of their own conceptual change. Especially powerful in social studies where students arrive with partial or distorted priors.

Step-by-step
  1. Students write: “I used to think ___. Now I think ___ because ___.”
  2. Optional turn-and-talk.
  3. Collect for next day's opener, or post anonymously.

Microlab Protocol

90 seconds each, uninterrupted. Silence while listening is the move.

Research & how it works

A National School Reform Faculty protocol. Gives every student equal airtime and builds listening as a discipline.

When it works

At the end of a heavy-content day (Middle Passage, Indian Removal, Douglass). Closes class with dignity.

Step-by-step
  1. Groups of three.
  2. Each person speaks 90s uninterrupted. Others listen silently.
  3. Rotate through all three.
  4. 2 minutes open discussion within the trio.
  5. No whole-class share unless volunteered.

Cross-cutting

Six Principles running beneath everything.

If you forget every protocol in this hub, keep these.

1

Rehearsal before public performance. Always.

Writing or pair-talk before whole-class share. This single principle explains most of the effect sizes in the research.

2

Sentence frames lower the language demand.

Especially valuable for multilingual learners, but GLAD's research shows they benefit everyone.

3

Wait time matters more than you think.

Rowe's research: 3 seconds after questions, and after responses, dramatically improves quality.

4

Decenter yourself gradually.

Applebee & Langer: strongest literacy outcomes came from classrooms with teachers talking less and students more, in structured ways.

5

Reflect on the discussion, not just the content.

Students learn to discuss by noticing what discussion is. Build in 2 minutes of “how did we do?”

6

Protocols are training wheels that stay on.

Well-structured protocols produce better discussions than unstructured ones, even with adults.
