# Building Classroom Discussion in Middle School Social Studies
### A research-backed starter guide for Alderwood MS Room 1610

---

## Why this document exists

You've noticed what the research confirms: cold-calling rarely rescues a disengaged classroom, small groups drift off task without scaffolds, and a handful of confident voices dominate while most students sit quiet. These are not personal failures of your students or your teaching. They are predictable outcomes of asking adolescents to perform public reasoning without the supports they need to do it well.

The good news is that the research on academic discussion is remarkably consistent. When middle schoolers are given structured rehearsal before they speak, clear norms for how to speak, and a legitimate reason to listen to each other, participation and achievement both rise, and the rise is steepest for students who were previously most silent. Applebee, Langer, Nystrand, and Gamoran's landmark 2003 study across 64 middle and high school classrooms found that discussion-based approaches were significantly related to literacy gains, and that these gains held for low-achieving students just as strongly as for high-achieving ones ([Applebee et al., 2003](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/00028312040003685)). The research on Project GLAD, which your district is already investing in, finds that its Guided Oral Practice strategies benefit all students, not only multilingual learners, especially in vocabulary acquisition and collaborative output ([Education Northwest, OCDE Project GLAD Study](https://projectgladstudy.educationnorthwest.org/)).

A brief note on cold-calling. A 2022 classroom observational study of 86 cold-call events across 15 middle school teachers found that cold-calling produces two sharply different outcomes depending on how it is framed: when it is warm and invitational, it can build participation; when it is used to catch inattentive students, it produces "superficial engagement" and makes students feel incompetent and anxious ([Minero, Edutopia summary, 2023](https://www.edutopia.org/article/does-cold-calling-work-heres-what-the-research-says/)). The stronger and more consistent finding in the research is that the structured alternatives covered below (Think-Pair-Share, quick-writes, rehearsal partners, and discussion protocols) lower anxiety while raising the quality of talk ([Mundelsee & Jurkowski, 2021](https://researchschool.org.uk/durrington/news/how-effective-is-think-pair-share); [Cooper et al., 2021](https://www.lifescied.org/doi/10.1187/cbe.20-08-0200)).

The organizing principle beneath everything below comes from Zaretta Hammond's synthesis of culturally responsive pedagogy and cognitive neuroscience: students cannot engage in rigorous public thinking when their amygdala is in threat mode. Every protocol here is designed to lower the threat and raise the cognitive demand at the same time ([Hammond, 2015, *Culturally Responsive Teaching and the Brain*](https://us.corwin.com/books/cultural-responsv-teaching-brain-241754)). Pair that with Jeff Zwiers' research at Stanford on academic conversations (back-and-forth dialogues in which students build, challenge, and negotiate ideas), and you have the whole game ([Zwiers & Crawford, 2011](https://www.routledge.com/Academic-Conversations-Classroom-Talk-that-Fosters-Critical-Thinking-and-Content-Understandings/Zwiers-Crawford/p/book/9781571108845)).

Each section below is sequenced from **foundational** (start here, works from day one) to **intermediate** (ready after a few weeks of foundational work) to **advanced** (student-led structures that require a culture of talk in place).

## 1. Starting Class with Engaging Conversations

The opening five minutes set the frame for everything that follows. The research consensus, echoed in Ritchhart and colleagues' *Making Thinking Visible* work, is that openers should do three things at once: surface prior knowledge, generate curiosity, and give every student something to say before anyone has to speak publicly ([Project Zero, Visible Thinking](https://pz.harvard.edu/projects/visible-thinking)).

### Foundational: See-Think-Wonder

**What the research says.** Developed by Harvard Project Zero's Visible Thinking research initiative, See-Think-Wonder builds observation-before-interpretation habits that are central to historical thinking. Project Zero's own classroom research reports gains on state and district tests in reading, writing, and social studies in classrooms using thinking routines consistently ([Ritchhart, Church, & Morrison, 2011, *Making Thinking Visible*](https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/see-think-wonder)).

**When it works.** Any day you have a compelling image, artifact, map, political cartoon, or painting to open a unit or lesson. It is especially powerful for Early Republic content, where political cartoons and contemporary paintings carry enormous interpretive freight.

**When it struggles.** With boring images or when students are rushed through observation. Give real time on "See."

**Step by step.**
1. Project a single image for silent viewing (60 seconds).
2. Students write or discuss in pairs: "What do you SEE?" Only observable details, no interpretation yet.
3. Then: "What do you THINK is going on?" Interpretation grounded in observation.
4. Then: "What does it make you WONDER?" Questions for inquiry.
5. Surface a few ideas from each column on the board. Use those as the bridge into the day's content.

**Resource.** [Project Zero See-Think-Wonder routine PDF](https://pz.harvard.edu/sites/default/files/See%20Think%20Wonder_2.pdf)

### Foundational: Quick-Write into Turn-and-Talk

**What the research says.** Quick-writes are one of the Himmeles' core Total Participation Techniques. They guarantee every student has something to say before any public sharing, which is the single most reliable mechanism for widening participation ([Himmele & Himmele, 2011, *Total Participation Techniques*](https://www.ascd.org/books/total-participation-techniques-third-edition)). Mundelsee and Jurkowski's 2021 study of 481 ninth graders found that students who had time to think and talk with a peer before whole-class sharing were 1.7 times more likely to volunteer than students asked to respond immediately, and reported lower anxiety ([summarized at Durrington Research School](https://researchschool.org.uk/durrington/news/how-effective-is-think-pair-share)).

**When it works.** Any day, any content. This is the workhorse.

**When it struggles.** When the prompt is closed-ended (single right answer). Use prompts that invite a stance.

**Step by step.**
1. Post a prompt tied to the day's lesson. Strong prompts ask students to take a position, make a prediction, or connect to prior knowledge. Example for your Haitian Revolution unit: "If you were enslaved on Saint-Domingue in 1791, what would you risk revolting over, and what would you not risk?"
2. Three minutes of silent writing. No discussion. Set a visible timer.
3. Two minutes of turn-and-talk with a designated partner. Students must share what they wrote, not improvise new thoughts.
4. Sample two or three pairs using "warm call" (see the whole-class section below). The written work has de-risked the public share.

### Intermediate: Chalkboard Splash

**What the research says.** Another Himmele TPT. It converts the opener into a whole-class formative assessment visible to everyone without anyone having to speak first ([Himmele & Himmele, 2017, Edutopia interview](https://www.edweek.org/teaching-learning/opinion-total-participation-techniques-an-interview-with-persida-william-himmele/2016/07)).

**When it works.** Reviewing prior day's content, surfacing prior knowledge before a new topic, or processing a shared experience like a documentary clip.

**Step by step.**
1. Pose a single open-ended prompt ("Write one sentence explaining why the Alien and Sedition Acts mattered").
2. Students write their response on a quarter sheet or sticky note.
3. They silently walk up and post their response on the board, wall, or chart paper.
4. Students then silently circulate and read, looking for patterns, disagreements, and surprising ideas.
5. Open the floor: "What did you notice? What do you still wonder?" Now the discussion has evidence.

### Advanced: Connect-Extend-Challenge

**What the research says.** Another Project Zero routine, designed to make students' shifting understanding visible to themselves and to you ([Project Zero, Connect-Extend-Challenge](https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/connect-extend-challenge)).

**When it works.** As an opener on day 2 or 3 of a unit, after students have encountered some content. Less useful on day 1 when there is nothing yet to connect to.

**Step by step.**
1. Students write or discuss: **Connect** ("How does what we studied yesterday connect to something you already knew?").
2. **Extend** ("What new idea or direction does it push your thinking toward?").
3. **Challenge** ("What is still confusing, troubling, or hard to accept?").
4. The "Challenge" responses are gold for whole-class discussion, because they are authentic questions, not teacher-generated ones.

### Building up over the year
Start with quick-writes paired with turn-and-talks in the first two weeks. Once the routine is automatic, introduce See-Think-Wonder as the visual-source opener. Chalkboard Splash and Connect-Extend-Challenge enter once students are reliably writing for two to three minutes without prompting.

## 2. Pair and Small Group Work (Pairs and Trios)

Small groups drift off task when there is not enough structure or not enough legitimate reason for every member to contribute. The research on cooperative learning, going back to David and Roger Johnson's decades of work at the University of Minnesota, consistently identifies two features of groups that actually work: **positive interdependence** (the group cannot succeed unless every member contributes) and **individual accountability** (each student's contribution is visible and assessable) ([Johnson & Johnson, summarized at SERC Carleton](https://serc.carleton.edu/sp/library/sac/what.html)).

### Foundational: Think-Pair-Share with accountability

**What the research says.** Think-Pair-Share is the most-studied discussion protocol in K-12 research. It raises participation, lowers anxiety, and improves the quality of whole-class talk ([Lyman, 1981, University of Maryland; Reading Rockets overview](https://www.readingrockets.org/classroom/classroom-strategies/think-pair-share)). The catch, flagged by Cooper and colleagues in 2021, is that the "Share" phase tends to be dominated by the same voices unless the teacher explicitly structures accountability ([Cooper et al., 2021, *CBE Life Sciences Education*](https://www.lifescied.org/doi/10.1187/cbe.20-08-0200)).

**When it works.** Everywhere. Every day. The foundational move.

**When it struggles.** When students use the pair time to socialize rather than rehearse. Fix this by requiring a written artifact before the pair talks.

**Step by step.**
1. **Think** (silent write, 2-3 min). Prompt requires a stance or claim.
2. **Pair** (2 min). Partner A shares first for 60 seconds, partner B paraphrases and adds. Then switch. Use a timer.
3. **Share** with accountability: do not ask "who wants to share?" Instead: "Share what your *partner* said that made you think." This holds both listeners and speakers accountable.

**Resource.** The Himmeles' [Total Participation Techniques](https://www.totalparticipationtechniques.com/) site catalogs variants.

### Foundational: Accountable Talk sentence frames (from GLAD and Zwiers)

**What the research says.** Zwiers and Crawford identify five core conversation skills: elaborating on ideas, clarifying, supporting with evidence, building on and challenging others' ideas, and paraphrasing to summarize. Explicit sentence frames make these skills learnable instead of mysterious ([Zwiers & Crawford, 2011](https://jeffzwiers.org/academic-conversations)). This is also the heart of GLAD's Guided Oral Practice strategies, which research shows significantly improve vocabulary and oral output ([OCDE Project GLAD Study, Education Northwest](https://projectgladstudy.educationnorthwest.org/)).

**When it works.** Any paired or small-group discussion. Post the frames. Refer to them. Practice them explicitly.

**Starter set of frames (post these in Room 1610):**
- To add on: "I want to build on what _____ said. I think..."
- To respectfully disagree: "I see it differently because..."
- To ask for evidence: "What in the source makes you think that?"
- To paraphrase: "So what I hear you saying is..."
- To clarify: "Can you say more about what you mean by...?"

**Step by step.**
1. Introduce two frames at a time. Not all five at once.
2. Model them yourself. When you respond to a student, use the frame aloud.
3. Do one activity where students must use the target frames at least twice and tally-mark each use. The gamification matters.
4. After two weeks, add the next two frames.

### Intermediate: Trios with assigned roles

**What the research says.** Cooperative learning research consistently shows that assigned roles reduce off-task behavior and ensure distributed participation. Elizabeth Cohen's Complex Instruction work at Stanford demonstrated that strategic role assignment, combined with "assigning competence" to lower-status students, widens who speaks in groups ([Cohen et al., 1999, summarized in Cooper et al., 2021](https://www.lifescied.org/doi/10.1187/cbe.20-08-0200)).

**When it works.** For document analysis, source corroboration, and any task with three clear subtasks. Trios beat quads for middle school because there is nowhere to hide.

**When it struggles.** If roles rotate too rarely, students ossify into identities. Rotate every session.

**Step by step (example: analyzing three primary sources on the Haitian Revolution).**
1. Each student gets a role: **Sourcer** (who wrote this, when, why), **Evidencer** (what does the source say), **Connector** (how does this source fit with the others).
2. Each student reads all three sources but analyzes one through their assigned role's lens.
3. Group produces a single shared graphic organizer. No one leaves until all three perspectives are on the page.
4. Debrief: ask groups to report out not their own role, but what they learned from one of their groupmates' roles. This forces listening during the group work.

### Intermediate: Structured Academic Controversy (SAC) in small groups

**What the research says.** SAC, developed by David and Roger Johnson, is one of the best-researched cooperative learning structures specifically designed for controversial social studies content. It requires students to argue a position, then argue the opposite position, then drop the positions and reach consensus. Walter Parker at the University of Washington has spent thirty years refining SAC for civic education ([Parker, *Structured Academic Controversy: What It Can Be*](https://education.uw.edu/sites/default/files/284/Parker_SAC.pdf); [TeachingHistory.org SAC guide](https://teachinghistory.org/teaching-materials/teaching-guides/21731)).

**When it works.** Juicy historical questions with real evidence on both sides. Examples for your curriculum: "Was the Louisiana Purchase constitutional?" "Was the Whiskey Rebellion a legitimate protest or a threat to the new government?" "Did the Haitian Revolution succeed?"

**When it struggles.** With false-balance questions or topics where one side is morally indefensible. Pick controversies historians actually argue about.

**Step by step (in groups of four, paired into A-B / C-D).**
1. Teacher provides a binary question and two sets of source packets (Side A and Side B). About 15-20 minutes to read.
2. Pair A prepares Side A's strongest case. Pair C-D prepares Side B's. (5-10 min)
3. Pair A presents to Pair C-D for 3 minutes. Then C-D paraphrases A's position to A's satisfaction before presenting their own.
4. **Switch sides.** Pair A must now argue Side B, using C-D's evidence, and vice versa. This is the critical move.
5. Drop the roles. All four students now work together to reach consensus, a refined position that accounts for the strongest evidence on both sides.

**Resource.** [TeachingHistory.org SAC guide with a worked example on "Was Abraham Lincoln a racist?"](https://teachinghistory.org/teaching-materials/teaching-guides/21731)

### Advanced: Expert Groups / Jigsaw

**What the research says.** Jigsaw is a GLAD strategy and one of Slavin's most cited cooperative learning structures. Students become "experts" in a subtopic, then teach peers. The structure is both powerfully interdependent and deeply accountable ([Slavin, 1995, *Cooperative Learning*; OCDE Project GLAD Study](https://projectgladstudy.educationnorthwest.org/)).

**When it works.** When content can be legitimately divided into parallel parts. For Haitian Revolution: one group on Toussaint, one on Dessalines, one on the French response, one on international reactions.

**When it struggles.** When the subtopics are not actually parallel in difficulty, or when expert groups do not produce a shared artifact (notes, sketchnote, infographic) to carry back.

**Step by step.**
1. Form home groups of 3-4. Number off.
2. All the 1s go to Expert Group on subtopic A, 2s to B, etc.
3. Expert groups research together and build a shared explanation + visual. Give a template.
4. Experts return to home groups and teach. Home group must produce a synthesis.
5. Accountability: individual quiz, or home group presents a synthesis the teacher randomly assigns one member to deliver.

### Building up over the year
Start with Think-Pair-Share + sentence frames in week one. Roles enter in week three or four once students trust the rehearsal model. SAC typically works well by mid-year for middle schoolers who have been doing structured pairs regularly. Jigsaw is easier to implement mechanically, but harder to do well: it relies on the other group structures already functioning.

## 3. Whole-Class Discussions

Whole-class discussion is the hardest format to get right in middle school, and it is the one where the cold-call reflex bites hardest. The research-backed path forward has two pillars: (1) never ask students to enter whole-class talk without rehearsal, and (2) reduce the teacher's role from distributor-of-turns to facilitator-of-threads.

### Foundational: Warm-Cool Calling (the supportive cold-call)

**What the research says.** The 2022 observational study mentioned earlier found that the difference between cold-calling that damages classroom culture and cold-calling that builds it is entirely in framing and follow-up. The research-backed version is sometimes called warm-calling ([Edutopia, 2023](https://www.edutopia.org/article/does-cold-calling-work-heres-what-the-research-says/); [Edutopia, 2025](https://www.edutopia.org/article/cold-calling-classroom-tips-effective-use/)).

**When it works.** After every student has rehearsed their thinking in writing or in pairs. Never as a first move.

**When it struggles.** When students have not had rehearsal time, or when the call feels like a pop quiz.

**The research-backed protocol.**
1. Frame it publicly on day one: "In this classroom, I call on people by name because I want to hear from everyone, not just the same few voices. I will always give you time to think or write first, and there's no penalty for being wrong."
2. **Ask the question BEFORE naming the student.** Naming first causes everyone else to disengage. Doug Lemov's research on this is clear ([summarized at Edutopia](https://www.edutopia.org/article/does-cold-calling-work-heres-what-the-research-says/)).
3. Pause for 5-10 seconds of wait time. (Mary Budd Rowe's classic research on wait time found that extending from 1 second to 3+ seconds raises the length, quality, and volume of student responses; [Rowe, 1986](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/002248718603700110)).
4. Call on a student. If they pass, let them "phone a friend" and then come back to them with a paraphrase task ("What part of what Maya said do you agree with?"). This preserves their dignity while still holding them accountable.

### Foundational: Discussion-first questions vs. answer-first questions

**What the research says.** Zwiers distinguishes "conversation-worthy" prompts from "prove-you-know-it" prompts. Only the former generate sustained academic talk ([Zwiers, *Next Steps with Academic Conversations*](https://www.middleweb.com/42103/fresh-approaches-to-academic-conversations/)). In social studies, the best whole-class discussion questions have three features:
- **Real stakes** (there is something to argue about)
- **Textual grounding** (evidence can be marshaled)
- **Multiple defensible answers**

**Strong examples for your content:**
- "Was Thomas Jefferson a hypocrite?" (Declaration + slaveholding)
- "Should the U.S. have recognized Haitian independence in 1804?"
- "Was the Monroe Doctrine an act of protection or imperialism?"

**Weak examples (avoid for whole-class discussion, these are for quizzes):**
- "When was the Constitution ratified?"
- "What were the three branches of government?"

### Intermediate: Fishbowl

**What the research says.** Fishbowl dramatically lowers the social stakes of whole-class talk by making the "audience" the majority and the "discussion group" a small circle. Facing History's widely-used discussion strategy collection cites it as one of their core protocols for middle and high school ([Facing History & Ourselves, *Fishbowl Teaching Strategy*](https://www.facinghistory.org/resource-library/fishbowl)).

**When it works.** When you have a meaty open question and you want a subset of students modeling discourse while others observe and prepare.

**When it struggles.** When the outside circle has no job. Give them one.

**Step by step.**
1. Inner circle: 6-8 students who will discuss for 10-12 minutes.
2. Outer circle: everyone else. Each outer-circle student is assigned a specific inner-circle student to observe ("discussion partner"). Their job is to track what their person says and note moves they make (evidence citations, building on others, asking questions).
3. Inner circle discusses the prompt.
4. Swap. Inner circle becomes outer circle. New inner group discusses (you can repeat the same question or deepen it).
5. Debrief: each outer-circle observer debriefs with their inner-circle partner on what they noticed about their moves.

### Advanced: Socratic Seminar

**What the research says.** Socratic seminars have been studied extensively in middle and high school settings. The research is favorable when seminars are well-scaffolded: students prepare a text in advance, follow norms, and the teacher stays mostly silent ([Mintz, 2006, review in Schaik's *Effectiveness of the Socratic Method*](https://scholarcommons.sc.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1254&context=senior_theses); [Facing History Socratic Seminar guide](https://www.facinghistory.org/resource-library/socratic-seminar); [Edutopia, 2025, middle school Socratic seminars](https://www.edutopia.org/article/socratic-seminars-middle-school)).

A study at Cal State of Socratic seminars in AVID middle-school classrooms found significant gains in students' self-awareness, social awareness, and sense of peer connectedness ([Baray, 2022, Cal State ScholarWorks](https://scholarworks.calstate.edu/downloads/wp988r99w)).

**When it works.** After months of foundational work. Students need the sentence frames and the rehearsal habits already in place. Text selection matters enormously: pick a rich, ambiguous source that middle schoolers can genuinely engage with. For your content: Toussaint Louverture's 1801 Constitution, excerpts from the Declaration of Independence compared to the Haitian Declaration of 1804, or a Frederick Douglass speech on Haitian recognition.

**When it struggles.** When students are underprepared, when the text is too easy (no discussion needed), or when the teacher talks too much. The teacher's job is to shut up.

**Step by step.**
1. **Prep day** (in-class or homework): students read the text and prepare annotated responses + 2-3 open-ended questions of their own.
2. Come to class with their prep or they do not participate. Hold this line.
3. Physical setup: circle. Teacher outside the circle. Teacher does not call on anyone; students learn to read cues and make space.
4. **Norms posted:** talk to each other not the teacher, cite the text, build on others' ideas, invite quiet voices, one person speaks at a time.
5. Start with one open question. Let silence happen. Middle schoolers will fill it if you don't.
6. Teacher's only moves: tally participation (not to grade, to notice patterns), and redirect if norms slip.
7. **Reflection after:** written self-assessment and one goal for next time. Research consistently shows the reflection matters as much as the seminar itself.

**Variant for your first seminars.** Break into two smaller seminars of 12-15 students each (as Sarah Tantillo and Jamison Fort describe on [MiddleWeb](https://www.middleweb.com/23250/sarah-tweaks-her-socratic-seminar-recipe/)). You walk between them. Participation pressure drops, airtime per student rises.

### Building up over the year
Use warm-calling as your everyday whole-class move. Introduce Fishbowl in the second month, when students have the sentence frames internalized. Save Socratic Seminar for later in the year (quarter 2 or 3 at the earliest for 8th graders who haven't done it before), and scaffold heavily.

## 4. Closing Class

The close of class is where learning is either consolidated or lost. The research on formative assessment, most famously Black and Wiliam's foundational work showing that frequent formative feedback produces some of the largest effect sizes in education research, suggests that the final 3-5 minutes are disproportionately valuable if used to make thinking visible ([Black & Wiliam, 1998](https://www.gla.ac.uk/t4/learningandteaching/files/PGCTLHE/BlackandWiliam1998.pdf); Dylan Wiliam, *Embedded Formative Assessment*, 2011).

### Foundational: Exit Tickets (done right)

**What the research says.** Exit tickets are a variant of Angelo and Cross's "Minute Paper" Classroom Assessment Technique. When used iteratively and actually read, they improve both instruction (because they reveal misconceptions) and student metacognition ([Angelo & Cross, 1993, *Classroom Assessment Techniques*; iterative study in *College Teaching*, Tandfonline, 2024](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/87567555.2024.2355210)). The research consistently shows that exit tickets work best when their data actually informs the next day's lesson, not when they are collected and filed.

**Step by step.**
1. Pose a single focused prompt on the last 3-4 minutes of class. Not "what did you learn today" (too open), and not a recall question (too narrow). Aim for application or connection.
2. Students respond on a sticky, index card, or digital form.
3. Read them that evening (or during prep). Sort into 3 piles: **got it**, **partial**, **misconception**.
4. Open the next day by explicitly addressing what you found ("Yesterday several of you were wondering whether..."). This is the step most teachers skip and it is the one that makes exit tickets worth their time.

**Strong prompt types for social studies:**
- **Application to a new context:** "If Toussaint were alive in 1820s Mexico, which side would he take in the independence movements, and why?"
- **Connection:** "How does today's content connect to something from Unit 6?"
- **Stance with evidence:** "Do you agree that the Whiskey Rebellion was a failure? One sentence, one piece of evidence."
- **Question generation:** "What's one question you still have about the Early Republic?"

### Foundational: 3-2-1

**What the research says.** 3-2-1 is a widely-used exit-ticket variant specifically designed to support metacognition. Like other structured reflection routines, its value is in the self-noticing it prompts ([Hattie & Timperley, 2007, on the power of feedback](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/003465430298487)).

**Step by step.**
1. **Three** things you learned today.
2. **Two** connections you made (to other content, to your life, to the news).
3. **One** question you still have.

The "one question" pile becomes your opener for tomorrow. Connects beautifully to Connect-Extend-Challenge.

### Intermediate: I Used to Think... Now I Think...

**What the research says.** Another Project Zero Visible Thinking routine, specifically designed to capture shifts in understanding. This is especially powerful in social studies where students often arrive with partial or distorted prior understandings ([Project Zero, I Used to Think... Now I Think... routine](https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/i-used-to-think-now-i-think)).

**When it works.** At the end of a lesson or unit that meaningfully challenged a prior conception. The Haitian Revolution is a perfect fit: most students arrive knowing nothing or knowing distortions.

**Step by step.**
1. Students write: "I used to think ______ about _______. Now I think ______ because _______."
2. Optional: a brief turn-and-talk to share.
3. Collect a few for next day's opener, or post anonymously as a wall.

### Intermediate: Chalkboard Splash (closing version)

Same protocol as the opening version but shifted to the end. Prompt becomes a synthesis: "In one sentence, what's the most important thing someone should know about what we studied today?" Students post, circulate, notice.

### Advanced: Microlab Protocol (shared reflection)

**What the research says.** Microlab is a National School Reform Faculty protocol used in teacher professional development and adaptable for classrooms. It gives every student equal airtime and builds listening as a discipline ([NSRF Microlab Protocol](https://nsrfharmony.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/microlab_0.pdf)).

**When it works.** At the end of a heavy-content or emotionally-weighty day (a lesson on the Middle Passage, for example). Closes class with dignity and builds community.

**Step by step.**
1. Groups of three.
2. One prompt. Each person speaks for 90 seconds uninterrupted. Others listen silently, do not interrupt, do not respond. Timer matters.
3. Rotate: person 2 speaks for 90 seconds, person 3 for 90 seconds.
4. After all three have spoken, 2 minutes of open discussion within the trio.
5. No whole-class share unless students volunteer.

The silence-while-listening part is the pedagogical move. Middle schoolers find it hard at first and rewarding by the third time.

### Building up over the year
Exit tickets should be your workhorse starting week one. 3-2-1 and "I Used to Think... Now I Think..." enter in the first month. Microlab is a once-every-few-weeks tool, not a daily closer.

## A few cross-cutting principles

These show up in the research under many names but they are the same handful of moves:

1. **Rehearsal before public performance, always.** Writing or pair-talk before whole-class share. This single principle explains most of the effect sizes in the research above.

2. **Sentence frames reduce the language demand.** Especially valuable for multilingual learners, but GLAD's research finds they benefit everyone, and you already know this from your district training ([Education Northwest study on OCDE Project GLAD](https://educationnorthwest.org/sites/default/files/events/resources/impact-of-project-glad-paper-508.pdf)).

3. **Wait time matters more than you think.** Rowe's classic research found a 3-second pause after questions, and after student responses, dramatically improved response quality, length, and participation rates ([Rowe, 1986](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/002248718603700110)).

4. **The teacher's job is to decenter themselves gradually.** Applebee and Langer's work is clear that the classrooms with the strongest literacy outcomes had teachers talking less and students talking more, in structured ways ([Applebee et al., 2003](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/00028312040003685)).

5. **Reflect on the discussion, not just the content.** Students learn to discuss by noticing what discussion is. Build in 2 minutes of "how did we do as a discussing class today?" after major protocols.

6. **Protocols are training wheels that stay on.** The goal is not to abandon Think-Pair-Share in favor of "real" discussion. The research shows that structured protocols produce better discussions than unstructured ones even with adults.

## Suggested first-quarter progression

A possible pacing, with the understanding that your students, your content, and your read of the room will shape this:

**Weeks 1-2.** Quick-writes into Turn-and-Talks every day. Introduce two sentence frames. Establish warm-calling norms explicitly. Exit tickets daily.

**Weeks 3-4.** Add See-Think-Wonder as your visual-source opener. Introduce two more sentence frames. Trios with assigned roles for your first document analysis.

**Weeks 5-6.** Chalkboard Splash, Connect-Extend-Challenge. First Fishbowl on a genuinely contested question. 3-2-1 as a closer.

**Weeks 7-9.** First Structured Academic Controversy. Refine Fishbowl. Begin "I Used to Think... Now I Think..." reflections.

**End of Q1.** Students are ready for their first Socratic Seminar by Q2 if the foundational habits are strong.
